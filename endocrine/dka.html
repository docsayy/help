Introduction

Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) is a life‚Äëthreatening diabetic emergency. It is a severe metabolic complication of diabetes, typically seen in type 1 diabetes (new presentation, missed insulin, or intercurrent illness), but it can also occur in type 2 diabetes in specific settings.

Hyperglycemia causes osmotic diuresis leading to volume depletion and electrolyte abnormalities, requiring prompt recognition and management.

Epidemiology
  • DKA is a common acute hyperglycemic complication among type 1 diabetes patients.
  • Increasingly recognized in type 2 diabetes (see ‚ÄúUnusual presentations‚Äù).
  • Estimated ~4% of patients with T1DM develop DKA annually; up to ~14% of diabetes-related hospital admissions may be due to DKA.
Pathophysiology
Mechanism (stepwise) ‚åÑ
  • Insulin deficiency ‚Üí inability to utilize glucose ‚Üí rising serum glucose (hyperglycemia).
  • Glucose cannot be used ‚Üí ‚Üë hepatic glucose production via glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis.
  • Counter‚Äëregulatory hormones (e.g., cortisol, glucagon, growth hormone) rise ‚Üí worsens hyperglycemia and drives alternative fuel use.
  • ‚Üë Lipolysis ‚Üí ‚Üë free fatty acids ‚Üí hepatic ketogenesis.
  • Ketone bodies (acetone, acetoacetate, and predominantly Œ≤‚Äëhydroxybutyrate) rise ‚Üí ketonemia.
  • Ketones are weak acids ‚Üí metabolic acidosis; progressive illness with increasing ketone burden.
  • Hyperglycemia ‚Üí osmotic diuresis + vomiting ‚Üí profound hypovolemia ‚Üí electrolyte derangements, reduced consciousness, death if untreated.

Management
Goals of therapy ‚åÑ
  • Restore circulating volume and tissue perfusion
  • Clear ketones and halt ketogenesis
  • Decrease glucose toward normal
  • Correct electrolyte derangements (especially potassium)
  • Identify and treat the precipitant

Fluid resuscitation ‚åÑ

Patients are intravascularly depleted and require IV fluids. Assess volume clinically + with labs; consider Foley for accurate I/O when needed.

  • Initial fluid: 0.9% normal saline (especially if SBP < 90 mmHg).

Intravenous insulin therapy ‚åÑ

Start a fixed‚Äërate IV insulin infusion (FRIII concept) based on patient weight.

  • Insulin rate: 0.1 units/kg/hr (e.g., 60 kg ‚Üí 6 units/hr)
  • If patient uses long‚Äëacting basal insulin, continue basal during IV insulin; hold prandial/short‚Äëacting insulin.
  • Newly diagnosed: a basal insulin may be started at 0.25 units/kg SQ per protocol.
Dextrose ‚Äúcrossover‚Äù and insulin reduction ‚åÑ

When glucose falls below 250 mg/dL (14 mmol/L):

  • Start IV dextrose (e.g., D10 at ~125 mL/hr per referenced protocol) in addition to NS + insulin (does not replace NS).
  • Consider reducing insulin infusion to 0.05 units/kg/hr to prevent hypoglycemia and hypokalemia while continuing to clear ketones.

Key idea: you keep insulin running to stop ketogenesis; dextrose prevents hypoglycemia while ketones clear.

Resolution & transition
When to stop IV insulin ‚åÑ

Continue IV insulin until ketoacidosis has resolved:

  • Ketones < 0.6 mmol/L
  • Venous pH > 7.30 and/or bicarbonate > 18 mEq/L

If glucose < 250 mg/dL (14.0 mmol/L), continue D10 (or institutional equivalent) alongside IV insulin + NS ¬± KCl.

Transition to subcutaneous insulin ‚åÑ
  • Inform diabetes/endocrine team early; aim review within ~24 hours where available.
  • When resolved and patient is eating/drinking: transition to SQ insulin.
  • Overlap is mandatory: give SQ insulin with a meal, then stop IV insulin ~1 hour later.
  • If not eating/drinking: consider transition to a variable‚Äërate insulin infusion per institutional protocol.
Complications
Major complications and why they happen ‚åÑ
  • Hypokalemia (major cause of mortality): total body K depletion + insulin shift ‚Üí rapid drop.
  • ARDS and other critical illness complications.
  • Co‚Äëmorbid precipitants: sepsis or MI drive mortality risk.
  • Cerebral edema: more common in pediatrics; rare but catastrophic.
  • Hypoglycemia: from insulin without adequate dextrose crossover.
  • AKI: from severe dehydration (pre‚Äërenal injury); worsened by ongoing osmotic diuresis.

Potassium disturbances are central: dehydration ‚Üí AKI; acidosis ‚Üí transmembrane K shifts (often elevated at presentation); insulin therapy ‚Üí intracellular shift ‚Üí dangerous hypokalemia unless replaced.
