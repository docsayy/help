Definition & classification

Hyperglycaemic hyperosmolar state (HHS) is an acute diabetic emergency that occurs in patients with type 2 diabetes mellitus. HHS occurs insidiously over several days with dehydration and metabolic disturbances that are more extreme than diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA).

Characteristic features ‚åÑ
  • Hypovolaemia
  • Hyperglycaemia: > 540 mg/dL (> 30 mmol/L)
  • Mild or absent ketonaemia: blood ketones < 3 mmol/L
  • High osmolality: > 320 mOsm/kg

HHS is clinically distinct to DKA and requires a different approach to treatment. Patients are usually elderly with multiple co-morbidities, and as a result may be very unwell.

Previously termed Hyperglycaemic Hyperosmolar Non-ketotic Coma (HONK). Terminology changed as most patients do not present in coma, though they may be extremely unwell.

Epidemiology

HHS is a life-threatening condition, which usually occurs in the elderly but is increasingly recognised in younger patients.

  • Estimated to account for ~1% of diabetic hospital admissions.
  • Average age of presentation ~60 years.
  • Associated with 15‚Äì20% mortality.
  • Often the first presentation of type 2 diabetes mellitus in up to 20‚Äì30% of cases.
Pathophysiology
Mechanism‚åÑ

In HHS, the relative lack of insulin is coupled with a rise in counter-regulatory hormones (e.g. cortisol, growth hormone, glucagon) that leads to a profound rise in glucose. These patients retain a certain level of insulin, which prevents the development of ketosis that epitomises DKA. However, the level of insulin is inadequate to prevent profound hyperglycaemia.

The excessive glucose leads to massive osmotic diuresis within the kidneys with the loss of essential electrolytes such as sodium and potassium. This is because the proximal tubules within the kidneys only have a certain capacity for reabsorption of glucose. Once this is reached, the remaining glucose is passed through the renal nephrons causing diuresis.

As water is lost, there is profound dehydration and reduced circulating volume, resulting in hyperosmolarity and marked hyperglycaemia. Patients with HHS may have up to a 9 litre deficit of water.

Increased osmolality triggers compensatory mechanisms (ADH release and thirst), but if these cannot compensate (e.g. elderly patients with co-morbidities), hypovolaemia develops with progression to acute kidney injury, electrolyte disturbances, hypotension and coma.

The hyperosmolar state leads to hyperviscosity, increasing the risk of arterial and venous thrombosis (e.g. stroke, DVT).

HHS pathophysiology
Precipitants

A number of underlying conditions are known to precipitate HHS (many cases are new T2DM diagnoses).

  • Infection
  • High-dose steroids
  • Myocardial infarction
  • Vomiting
  • Stroke
  • Poor treatment concordance
Clinical features
Symptoms & signs ‚åÑ

Onset is usually insidious with dehydration over days to weeks. Early features may include polyuria, polydipsia and nausea; late features include altered mental status, seizures and possibly coma.

  • Polyuria
  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Muscle cramps
  • Weakness
  • Altered mental status

  • Decreased conscious level
  • Coma
  • Focal neurology signs

Management
Core goals ‚åÑ

Management facets: aggressive fluid resuscitation plus normalization of glucose and osmolality.

  • Normalise osmolality
  • Normalise blood glucose
  • Replace fluid and electrolytes
  • Prevention of arterial/venous thrombosis
  • Prevention of complications & foot ulceration
HHS management algorithm
Initial assessment (ABCDE) ‚åÑ

All patients should undergo ABCDE assessment with formal GCS and full observations.

  • IV access (x2 large-bore cannula)
  • Blood/urinary ketones
  • Capillary & plasma blood glucose
  • CBC, electrolytes (BMP/CMP), venous blood gas, plasma osmolality
  • Blood cultures
  • Urinalysis ¬± MSU, pregnancy test (as indicated)
  • ECG
  • CXR
  • Urinary catheter (for accurate monitoring when needed)
  • Additional tests as indicated (e.g. troponin, CT head)
Severity
Markers of severity (higher level of care) ‚åÑ

Patients with HHS are often elderly with multiple co-morbidities and can be extremely unwell. Ideally, manage in a high-dependency environment (level 2 care and above).

Markers that warrant high-level care:

  • Osmolality > 350 mOsm/kg
  • Sodium > 160 mEq/L (160 mmol/L)
  • pH < 7.1
  • GCS < 12
  • Systolic BP < 90 mmHg
  • Serum creatinine > 2.26 mg/dL (> 200 Œºmol/L)
  • Macrovascular event (e.g., MI, CVA)
  • Severe electrolyte abnormalities (e.g., hyper/hypokalaemia)
Intravenous fluids
Fluid strategy (slow correction) ‚åÑ

Patients with HHS can have a tremendous fluid deficit. Initial management requires fluid resuscitation to restore circulating volume. Initial fluid of choice is 0.9% sodium chloride (normal saline); give at least 1 liter over 1 hour (faster if significant hypotension).

Further fluids are guided by urine output and clinical response. A proposed target is +2‚Äì3 liters by 6 hours.

Normal saline may cause a transient rise in sodium; if osmolality is falling appropriately this is not an indication for hypotonic saline (e.g., 0.45%). Rapid correction is not advisable because of osmolar shifts leading to cerebral oedema ‚Äî generally aim for around +4 liters in the first 24 hours.

Insulin therapy
When to start insulin (delayed and selective) ‚åÑ

Insulin use in HHS should be led by a specialist or senior clinician with experience of HHS management.

  • Start insulin only if there is evidence of significant ketonaemia (> 1 mmol/L) or ketonuria (2+ or more).
  • Another indication is when glucose is falling less than 90 mg/dL/hr (5 mmol/L/hr).
  • Assess and ensure adequate fluid balance before prescribing insulin.
Fixed-rate IV insulin infusion (FRIII concept) ‚åÑ
  • If indicated: FRIII at 0.05 units/kg/hr (half the dose used in DKA).
  • Maintain glucose between 180‚Äì270 mg/dL (10‚Äì15 mmol/L) in the first 24 hours.
  • Use 5% or 10% dextrose if levels fall 252 mg/dL (14 mmol/L).
Practical warning (why the delay matters): In HHS, rapid glucose reduction can drive a rapid drop in plasma osmolality ‚Üí cerebral oedema risk. Fluids are the primary therapy early.
Electrolyte replacement
Electrolytes + potassium rules ‚åÑ

Electrolytes including sodium, potassium, phosphate and magnesium should be monitored regularly (minimum q4h) and replaced as necessary. Potassium is especially important if insulin is started, because insulin drives potassium intracellularly ‚Üí low plasma potassium ‚Üí dangerous arrhythmias.

Monitoring
Monitoring schedule and safety checks ‚åÑ

Patients should be on cardiac monitoring and assessed at regular intervals.

  • Every hour: blood glucose, urea & electrolytes, and laboratory or calculated plasma osmolality for first 6 hours.
  • If satisfactory fall in osmolality (3‚Äì8 mOsm/kg/hr) and glucose (‚â• 5 mmol/L/hr), blood sampling can be reduced to 2-hourly.
  • Maintain accurate fluid balance; urine output documented hourly.
  • As parameters improve: reduce to 4-hourly, then 12-hourly monitoring.
  • Continuously assess for complications (stroke, DVT, cerebral oedema) and treat precipitant (infection, MI, etc.).
Metabolic treatment targets
Targets (what ‚Äúgood progress‚Äù looks like) ‚åÑ

Two main metabolic treatment targets should be achieved:

  • Plasma osmolality: falling by 3‚Äì8 mOsm/kg/hr
  • Blood glucose: falling by at least 90 mg/dL/hr (5 mmol/L/hr)
Continuing care
Specialist involvement + prevention steps ‚åÑ
  • Inform specialist diabetes team; ideally assess within 24 hours.
  • Start prophylactic LMWH unless contraindicated (high thrombotic risk).
  • Regular foot assessment for ulceration; encourage early mobilization; remove catheter when appropriate.
  • After initial phase: minimum daily urinalysis, electrolytes, and regular capillary glucose.
  • Ongoing need for FRIII vs transition to SQ insulin is managed with specialist team.
  • If FRIII started: do not stop until glucose is within target and patient is over acute episode.
  • If still not tolerating oral intake: may convert to VRIII in interim period (per institutional protocol).
Complications
Major complications (disease + treatment related) ‚åÑ
  • Myocardial infarction: hypovolaemia, hyperviscosity and severe illness strain the heart (often with pre-existing CAD).
  • Thrombotic complications: hyperviscosity + hypovolaemia predispose to DVT, PE, stroke. Prophylactic LMWH reduces risk if no contraindications.
  • Cerebral oedema: may occur from rapid correction of hyperglycaemia with rapid fall in plasma osmolarity ‚Üí headache, reduced GCS, and untreated can lead to death.
