1 Rhythm & sinus check

Ask yourself:
  • Is there a P wave before each QRS?
  • Are P waves upright in leads I and II?
  • Are PR and RR intervals reasonably regular?
If yes, you likely have sinus rhythm. If not, think atrial fibrillation/flutter, junctional, ventricular, or paced rhythms.

2 Heart rate

For a regular rhythm, use the big-box method:
  • Heart rate ‚âà 300 √∑ number of large boxes between QRS peaks.
  • Landmarks: 1 box ‚âà 300, 2 boxes ‚âà 150, 3 ‚âà 100, 4 ‚âà 75, 5 ‚âà 60, 6 ‚âà 50 bpm.
For irregular rhythms, count complexes in 10 seconds and multiply by 6.

3 P waves & atrial size

Look at II, III, aVF and V1.
  • Right atrial enlargement: tall, peaked P (>2.5 mm) in II/III/aVF (‚ÄúP pulmonale‚Äù).
  • Left atrial enlargement: broad, notched P in II (‚ÄúP mitrale‚Äù); in V1, a biphasic P with a deep, wide terminal negative component.

4 PR interval

Measure from P onset to QRS onset.
  • Normal ‚âà 0.12‚Äì0.20 s (3‚Äì5 small boxes).
  • >0.20 s with each P followed by QRS ‚Üí first-degree AV block.
  • Very short PR (<0.12 s) with normal QRS ‚Üí consider pre-excitation (e.g., LGL).
  • Short PR plus slurred upstroke of QRS (delta wave) ‚Üí think WPW.

5 QRS axis (frontal plane)

A quick trick:
  • If leads I and II are mostly positive ‚Üí normal axis.
  • I positive, II negative ‚Üí left axis deviation.
  • I negative, aVF positive ‚Üí right axis deviation.
  • I negative, aVF negative ‚Üí ‚Äúextreme‚Äù / northwest axis.
Combine axis with other findings:
  • RBBB + left axis deviation ‚Üí consider bifascicular block (RBBB + left anterior fascicle block).
  • LVH + right axis deviation ‚Üí think biventricular hypertrophy.

6 QRS duration & bundle branch blocks

  • Narrow QRS: <0.10 s.
  • Slightly wide: 0.10‚Äì0.12 s (may be incomplete bundle branch block).

8 Voltage & ventricular hypertrophy

Typical LVH clues:
  • Tall S in V1 + tall R in V5 or V6 with large combined voltage (depending on criteria used).
  • Often accompanied by ST depression / T inversion (‚Äústrain‚Äù).
Typical RVH clues:
  • Tall R in V1 with R/S > 1, deep S in V5‚ÄìV6.
  • Right axis deviation strengthens suspicion.
Low voltage:
  • QRS amplitude <0.5 mV in limb leads; consider pericardial effusion, COPD, obesity, etc.

When voltage matters

  • LVH + strain ‚Üí chronic pressure load (HTN, AS).
  • RVH + RAD ‚Üí pulmonary hypertension, chronic lung disease.
  • Low voltage + tachycardia ‚Üí think tamponade.

9 Infarction signs & localization

Look for:
  • New pathologic Q waves (wide and deep).
  • Poor R progression or QS complexes in precordial leads.
Territorial mapping:
  • II, III, aVF ‚Üí inferior wall.
  • V1‚ÄìV4 ‚Üí anterior / septal.
  • I, aVL, V5‚ÄìV6 ‚Üí high lateral / lateral.
  • Posterior: ST depression V1‚ÄìV3 with tall R (mirror image).

10 ST segment & T waves

Important patterns:
  • Localized, convex ST elevation in a coronary territory ‚Üí think acute transmural ischemia (STEMI).
  • ST depression / T inversion ‚Üí subendocardial ischemia, NSTEMI, or unstable angina.
  • Diffuse concave ST elevation with PR changes ‚Üí pericarditis / myopericarditis.
  • ST & T changes in LVH or bundle branch block are more difficult to interpret; report them as ‚Äúsecondary repolarization changes.‚Äù

11 QT interval & U waves

A quick bedside check:
  • Find one RR interval and ‚Äúhalve‚Äù it visually. If the T wave ends before halfway ‚Üí QT is probably acceptable.
  • If the T wave ends after halfway ‚Üí QT may be prolonged; verify with machine-calculated QTc.
Electrolyte patterns:
  • Hyperkalemia ‚Üí tall, peaked T waves; with severity, P disappears and QRS widens.
  • Hypokalemia ‚Üí flattened T, prominent U wave, ST depression.
  • Hypocalcemia ‚Üí prolonged QT; hypercalcemia ‚Üí shortened QT.
