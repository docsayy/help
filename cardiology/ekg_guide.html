5 QRS axis

Axis helps localize pathology and reflects chronic pressure, conduction disease, or prior infarction.
  • I & II positive → normal axis
  • I positive, II negative → left axis deviation
  • I negative, aVF positive → right axis deviation
  • I negative, aVF negative → extreme axis

6 QRS duration & bundle branch block

QRS width tells you whether depolarization is using the His–Purkinje system or spreading through myocardium.
  • <0.10 s: narrow
  • 0.10–0.12 s: incomplete BBB
  • ≥0.12 s: complete BBB

7 Precordial transition

Precordial transition reflects ventricular orientation and may suggest chamber enlargement or lead misplacement.
  • Normal transition: V3–V4
  • Late transition: clockwise rotation
  • Early transition: counterclockwise rotation

8 Voltage & hypertrophy

Voltage patterns suggest chronic pressure or volume overload but must be interpreted clinically.
  • LVH: high voltage ± strain
  • RVH: tall R in V1 with RAD
  • Low voltage: effusion, COPD, obesity

9 Infarction & localization

This step asks whether myocardial necrosis is present and where it is located.
  • Inferior: II, III, aVF
  • Anterior: V1–V4
  • Lateral: I, aVL, V5–V6

10 ST segment & T waves

ST–T abnormalities reflect ischemia, injury, or secondary repolarization changes.
  • Convex ST elevation: STEMI
  • ST depression/T inversion: ischemia
  • Diffuse concave ST elevation: pericarditis

11 QT interval & electrolytes

QT abnormalities predict arrhythmia risk and often reflect electrolyte or medication effects.
  • Hyperkalemia: peaked T waves
  • Hypokalemia: U waves
  • Hypocalcemia: prolonged QT
