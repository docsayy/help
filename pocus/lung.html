1. Basic Landmarks ‚Äì ‚ÄúBatwing Sign‚Äù

Place the probe in an intercostal space in the anterior chest (usually 2nd‚Äì4th intercostal space, mid-clavicular). You should see two rib shadows with the pleural line between them ‚Äì this is the classic ‚Äúbatwing sign‚Äù.

Key idea: Always identify ribs & pleural line first. Everything else (A-lines, B-lines, lung sliding) is interpreted relative to this line.

2. Lung Sliding & Seashore / Barcode Sign

What is lung sliding and why does it occur?

Lung sliding is the shimmering, to-and-fro motion of the visceral pleura against the parietal pleura with each breath. It requires:

  • The lung to be inflated and opposed to the chest wall
  • No significant air or thick fluid separating pleural layers
  • At least some tidal ventilation in that region

Seashore Sign (Normal) vs Barcode/Stratosphere Sign (Absent Sliding)

On M-mode, place the M-mode cursor through the pleural line:

  • Seashore sign: static chest wall above (horizontal lines) and speckled granular pattern below the pleura (moving lung) ‚Äì suggests normal lung sliding.
  • Barcode / Stratosphere sign: only parallel horizontal lines above and below the pleural line, indicating no motion ‚Äì seen in pneumothorax and also in apnea, mainstem intubation, lung overdistension, pleural adhesions, or very shallow breathing.
Clinical note: Absence of lung sliding is not specific for pneumothorax. Combine with A-line pattern, lung point, and clinical context.

3. A-Lines ‚Äì Reverberation of Normal Aerated Lung

A-lines are horizontal, regularly spaced reverberation artifacts that repeat at equal intervals below the pleural line. They occur because ultrasound reflects back and forth between the pleura and the probe over highly aerated lung.

A predominance of A-lines usually indicates normal lung aeration or conditions with hyperinflation / air trapping (COPD, asthma) or pneumothorax (when combined with absent sliding).

Think A-lines: normal lung, COPD/asthma, early/compensated ARDS in spared regions, or pneumothorax (if no lung sliding and possibly lung point).

4. Posterolateral Alveolar and/or Pleural Syndrome (PLAPS) & Curtain Sign

The PLAPS point is the dependent, posterolateral region where effusions, consolidations, and atelectasis frequently collect. Scan the right and left PLAPS points in semirecumbent or supine patients.

Curtain Sign

Over the diaphragm and upper abdomen, the aerated lung creates a moving ‚Äúcurtain‚Äù that covers and uncovers the liver or spleen during inspiration. This is the curtain sign, and it implies an aerated, normally expanding lung base.

Loss of curtain sign at the base suggests consolidation, effusion, or diaphragmatic paralysis preventing the lung from moving over the abdominal organs.

5. B-Lines ‚Äì Why They Happen & How to Interpret Them

What is a B-line and why does it occur?

B-lines are vertical, hyperechoic artifacts that:

  • Originate at the pleural line
  • Extend to the bottom of the screen without fading
  • Move synchronously with lung sliding
  • Erase A-lines

They represent ultrasound scattering from thickened interlobular septa or increased fluid in the subpleural interstitium and alveoli ‚Äì essentially an interstitial or alveolar-interstitial syndrome.

Interpretation depends on the pattern:

  • Isolated/Basal B-lines: may be normal, or due to dependent atelectasis, mild edema.
  • Focal B-lines: near a consolidation or focal pneumonia.
  • Diffuse, bilateral B-lines: consider cardiogenic pulmonary edema or ARDS; integrate with lung sliding, PLAPS findings, and clinical context.

6. Consolidation & Subpleural Foci ‚Äì ‚ÄúShred Sign‚Äù

When alveoli fill with fluid, pus, or cells, the lung becomes tissue-like and ‚Äúhepatized.‚Äù On ultrasound, this appears as a consolidation with a tissue echotexture similar to liver.

Shred Sign

The shred sign is the irregular, shredded deep border between consolidated lung and aerated lung. It reflects an uneven transition zone between solid and aerated tissue, typical of pneumonia.

Think pneumonia when you see a consolidation with shred sign, focal B-lines, and dynamic air bronchograms, especially at a PLAPS point.

7. Air Bronchograms ‚Äì Dynamic vs Static

Air bronchograms are hyperechoic, branching foci of air within consolidated lung tissue.

  • Static air bronchograms: do not move with respiration ‚Äì seen in dense atelectasis where air is trapped and no longer ventilated.
  • Dynamic air bronchograms: move with inspiration and expiration, reflecting air being pushed in and out of patent bronchi within consolidation ‚Äì highly suggestive of pneumonia.
Key distinction: Dynamic air bronchogram ‚Üí pneumonia; static air bronchogram ‚Üí atelectasis (e.g., from obstruction or compression).

8. Pleural Effusion on Ultrasound

Scan the posterior or lateral bases (PLAPS points). Pleural effusions appear as anechoic or hypoechoic fluid between the parietal and visceral pleura. Multiple classic signs help confirm and characterize effusions.

Key Pleural Effusion Signs

8.1 Spine Sign

Normally, the spine is only seen below the diaphragm. When fluid is present above the diaphragm, the spine can be traced higher into the thorax ‚Äì the spine sign.

8.2 Jellyfish Sign

The partially collapsed lung flapping within surrounding pleural fluid resembles a jellyfish.

8.3 Sinusoid Sign

On M-mode, the visceral pleura/lung tissue oscillates toward and away from the parietal pleura inside an effusion, creating a sinusoidal pattern.

8.4 Quad Sign

The effusion is bounded by the pleural line, lung line, and two rib shadows, forming a quadrilateral.

8.5 Plankton Sign

Low-amplitude echogenic particles moving within the effusion (protein, cells, debris) give a ‚Äúplankton‚Äù appearance, often indicating an exudative or complicated effusion.

8.6 Hematocrit Sign

Layering echogenic material with a dependent-fluid level can indicate a hemothorax or very dense exudate.

8.7 Loculated Pleural Effusions

Septations or loculations within the pleural space suggest complicated parapneumonic effusion or empyema. They may affect drainage strategy and chest tube placement.

9. Pneumothorax ‚Äì Ultrasound Profile

Ultrasound is more sensitive than chest X-ray for detecting pneumothorax in supine or critically ill patients. Scan anteriorly in a supine patient, moving laterally as needed.

Key Findings

  • No lung sliding on B-mode at an anterior position
  • Barcode / stratosphere sign on M-mode
  • A-lines pattern (highly aerated lung)
  • Lung point ‚Äì transition point where sliding reappears (very specific)

Lung Point

The lung point is the location where pneumothorax air intermittently gives way to normal apposed lung with sliding during the respiratory cycle. It is considered highly specific for pneumothorax if found.

Summary for pneumothorax: No lung sliding + A-lines + barcode sign suggestive. Finding a lung point is highly specific.

10. Ultrasound Profile of Pneumonia

Pneumonia often localizes to dependent or posterior lung regions and is well captured at PLAPS points.

  • Consolidation or hepatization of lung (often wedge-shaped, abutting pleura)
  • Subpleural consolidations in more peripheral disease
  • Shred sign at deep border between consolidated and aerated lung
  • B-lines: unilateral (bacterial) or bilateral (viral or multifocal)
  • Dynamic air bronchograms within consolidation
  • Possible small pleural effusion adjacent to the consolidation
  • Reduced lung sliding over areas of dense pleural thickening in severe disease
Pneumonia profile: Consolidation + shred sign + dynamic air bronchograms ¬± small effusion, usually focal.
