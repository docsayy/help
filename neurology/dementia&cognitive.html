Dementia & Cognitive Disorders

Stepwise differentiation, localization, and what actually matters clinically

Core Definitions

  • Dementia: chronic, progressive cognitive decline interfering with daily function.
  • Delirium: acute, fluctuating disturbance of attention and awareness.
  • Depression: cognitive complaints without true neurodegeneration (“pseudodementia”).
First rule: You cannot diagnose dementia until delirium is excluded.

Step 1: Dementia vs Delirium vs Depression

Delirium
Onset
Acute (hours–days), fluctuating.
Core feature
Inattention.
Common causes
Infection, meds, metabolic derangements, hypoxia.
Delirium is a medical emergency — find and fix the cause.
Depression
Pattern
“I don’t know” answers, prominent mood symptoms.
Testing
Effort inconsistent; memory improves with cueing.
Depression mimics dementia but does not destroy cortical functions.
Dementia
Pattern
Insidious onset, steadily progressive.
Key feature
Multiple cognitive domains affected.

Step 2: Cortical vs Subcortical Clues

Cortical Dementia
  • Early memory loss, aphasia, apraxia, agnosia
  • Disorientation, poor insight
Cortex problems → language, recognition, learned skills.
Subcortical Dementia
  • Psychomotor slowing
  • Executive dysfunction
  • Preserved language early
Subcortex problems → speed, motivation, executive control.

Major Dementia Syndromes

AD Alzheimer Disease
Pattern
Gradual episodic memory loss → language/executive decline.
Localization
Medial temporal → parietal lobes.
Imaging
MRI: hippocampal atrophy.
VaD Vascular Dementia
Pattern
Stepwise decline; executive dysfunction > memory.
Clue
Stroke history, focal signs.
Imaging
MRI: infarcts, white matter disease.
DLB Dementia with Lewy Bodies
Triad
Visual hallucinations, cognitive fluctuations, parkinsonism.
Danger
Severe neuroleptic sensitivity.
Avoid typical antipsychotics if possible.
FTD Frontotemporal Dementia
Pattern
Early personality, behavior, or language change.
Age
Often <65.
Imaging
Frontal/anterior temporal atrophy.

Step 3: What to Order (Once)

  • CBC, CMP
  • TSH
  • Vitamin B12 (± folate)
  • Consider RPR, HIV if risk factors
  • MRI brain (preferred) or CT if MRI unavailable
Routine dementia labs are about finding reversible causes — not confirming Alzheimer’s.

When to Suspect Reversible or Unusual Causes

  • Rapid progression (weeks–months)
  • Early gait disturbance or incontinence (NPH)
  • Prominent psychiatric or visual symptoms early
  • Young onset (<60)

When to Call Neurology

  • Diagnostic uncertainty
  • Atypical or rapidly progressive dementia
  • Young-onset cognitive decline
  • Concern for inflammatory, autoimmune, or prion disease
