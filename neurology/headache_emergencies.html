Headache Emergencies

Red flags, localization clues, and what to order first

Core Principle

Most headaches are benign — neurology gets involved when the headache suggests bleeding, infection, inflammation, mass effect, or pressure disorders.

New headache + abnormal exam = secondary headache until proven otherwise.

Step 1: Identify Red Flags SNOOP

  • Systemic illness: fever, weight loss, cancer, immunosuppression
  • Neurologic deficit: focal signs, AMS, seizure
  • Onset sudden: “thunderclap”
  • Older age >50 with new headache
  • Pattern change or progressive
Any SNOOP feature → imaging before reassurance.

Thunderclap Headache Rule out SAH

Think Subarachnoid Hemorrhage
Clues
  • Instant maximal pain (“worst headache of life”)
  • Neck stiffness, photophobia
  • Vomiting or collapse
First test
Noncontrast CT head (best early)
If CT negative
LP (xanthochromia) or CTA depending on timing and suspicion
Never discharge a true thunderclap headache without ruling out SAH.

Headache + Fever / AMS Infection

Think Meningitis / Encephalitis
Clues
  • Fever, neck stiffness
  • Altered mental status
  • New seizures
Next steps
  • Blood cultures
  • CT head first if focal deficit, seizure, immunocompromised
  • LP when safe
Start empiric antimicrobials early — don’t wait for LP perfection.

Headache + Vision Symptoms

Think Temporal Arteritis (GCA)
  • Age >50, new headache
  • Jaw claudication, scalp tenderness
  • Vision loss or diplopia
High ESR/CRP supports diagnosis, but treat immediately if suspected.
Think Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension
  • Headache worse lying flat
  • Pulsatile tinnitus
  • Transient visual obscurations
  • Papilledema on exam
MRI/MRV → LP with opening pressure.

Headache + Focal Deficit

Think Intracerebral Hemorrhage / Mass
  • Progressive headache
  • New focal neuro signs
  • Vomiting, decreased consciousness
Noncontrast CT is the fastest way to identify bleeding.
Think Cerebral Venous Sinus Thrombosis
  • Headache + seizures
  • Hypercoagulable states, pregnancy/postpartum
  • Can mimic SAH or tumor
Order MRV or CTV — plain CT can be normal.

When to Call Neurology

  • Thunderclap headache or suspected SAH
  • Headache with focal deficit or AMS
  • Suspected meningitis/encephalitis
  • Unclear diagnosis after initial imaging
