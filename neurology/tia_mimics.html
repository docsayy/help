TIA vs Mimics

How to decide “true transient ischemia” vs migraine/seizure/syncope and what to do next

Home Back to Neurology

What Is a TIA (Clinically)?

A TIA is a transient focal neurologic deficit due to ischemia without persistent infarction. Clinically: treat it like a stroke warning shot until proven otherwise.

Core rule: Focal symptoms that are sudden and “negative” (loss of function) are more TIA-like than spreading “positive” symptoms.

Step 1: The 60-Second “TIA Likelihood” Screen

TIA more likely
  • Sudden onset at a clear time
  • Negative symptoms: weakness, numbness, aphasia, vision loss
  • All symptoms start together (not marching/spreading)
  • Vascular risk factors (HTN, DM, AF, smoking)
Mimic more likely
  • Positive symptoms: tingling that spreads, flashing lights, shimmering vision
  • Gradual build over minutes with “march” (migraine aura)
  • Recurrent stereotyped brief episodes (focal seizures)
  • Loss of consciousness as primary feature (syncope)

Common Mimics (How to Tell)

Mimic Clues Why it’s not TIA
Migraine aura Gradual spread over minutes; visual scintillation; sensory march Positive symptoms + progression pattern
Focal seizure Stereotyped episodes; automatisms; post-event confusion Electrical event; may have Todd’s paralysis after
Syncope Lightheadedness, pallor, diaphoresis; LOC; rapid recovery Global hypoperfusion, not focal deficit
Hypoglycemia Sweats, tremor, confusion; improves with glucose Metabolic; can mimic focal deficits
Functional Inconsistent exam; non-anatomic pattern Diagnosis of exclusion
“Marching” symptoms (visual → sensory → speech) = migraine aura until proven otherwise. “All-at-once loss” = vascular until proven otherwise.

Step 2: High-Risk TIA Features (Admit / urgent workup)

High risk Red flags
  • Ongoing or recurrent symptoms (“crescendo” events)
  • Weakness or aphasia
  • Suspected posterior circulation symptoms with inability to walk
  • New atrial fibrillation or known cardioembolic source
  • Carotid bruit / suspected high-grade stenosis
  • Anticoagulation questions or high bleeding risk decisions
Posterior circulation TIAs can look “vague” but can precede basilar events. Do not dismiss persistent gait inability.

Step 3: What to Order (Practical Workup)

Brain Imaging
  • Non-contrast CT head (initial bleed exclusion if acute presentation)
  • MRI brain with DWI preferred when available (identifies small infarcts)
Vessels (look for stenosis/LVO)
  • CTA head/neck or carotid ultrasound (institution dependent)
  • Consider posterior circulation imaging if symptoms suggest it
Heart (look for embolic source)
  • ECG for AF/flutter
  • Telemetry when indicated
  • Echocardiography based on suspicion/protocol
Labs
  • Glucose, CBC, CMP, lipids, A1c (risk and mimics)

Step 4: Immediate Management (General Principles)

  • Treat as vascular until proven otherwise (risk modification starts now)
  • Start antiplatelet therapy per local stroke protocol if no contraindication
  • Statin and BP optimization are often indicated (protocol-based)
  • Anticoagulation decisions require careful stroke-team guidance when AF suspected/confirmed
Your job is not to “score and discharge.” Your job is to prevent the next one, which may not be transient.

When to Call Neurology

  • Any suspected TIA with weakness, aphasia, or posterior circulation symptoms
  • Recurrent transient deficits (“crescendo TIAs”)
  • Diagnostic uncertainty between seizure/migraine vs TIA
  • High-risk imaging findings or need for urgent secondary prevention plan
