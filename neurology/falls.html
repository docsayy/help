Falls & Recurrent Falls

Mechanical vs medical, older vs younger, and a history-first method to find the cause

Home Back to Neurology

Core Principle

A “fall” is not a diagnosis. The high-yield question is: Was this a mechanical fall… or a medical event that caused a fall?

Rule: If there was loss of consciousness, amnesia, palpitations, chest pain, seizure features, or focal neuro symptoms → treat as medical until proven otherwise.

Step 1: Stabilize & Screen for High-Risk Injury

Step 0 Immediate priorities
  • Vitals, glucose, O₂ sat
  • Assess head/neck/hip pain; anticoagulation status
  • Check for focal deficits; new confusion (delirium)
  • ECG early (arrhythmia clues)
Never miss: head bleed (especially anticoagulated), C-spine injury, hip fracture.

Step 2: Classify the Event (History-Based)

You can classify most falls in 60 seconds with the right questions.

A “Mechanical” fall (trip/slip) environmental
Key story
Tripped over object, slipped on surface, missed step; patient remembers the entire event.
Usually
No prodrome, no LOC, no confusion before fall.
But still ask
Why did they trip? Vision? Foot drop? Parkinsonism? Sedatives?
“Mechanical” is not the end — it often hides gait, vision, neuropathy, meds, or hypotension.
B Syncope / presyncope cardiac/orthostatic
Key story
Lightheaded, “blacked out,” woke up on ground; often amnesia for impact.
Prodrome?
Nausea, warmth, sweating (vasovagal) vs none (arrhythmia).
Red flags
No prodrome, exertional, supine, palpitations, chest pain, family SCD.
C Seizure epileptic
Key story
Witnessed convulsion, eye deviation, stereotyped movements, post-ictal confusion.
Supportive clues
Tongue bite (lateral), incontinence, prolonged confusion, muscle soreness.
Pitfall
Syncope can cause brief myoclonic jerks — focus on recovery time and post-ictal state.
D Stroke / TIA causing fall central
Key story
Sudden focal weakness, imbalance, diplopia, dysarthria, or new sensory loss → fall.
Posterior clues
“Dizzy + can’t walk,” vision symptoms, ataxia, cranial nerve findings.
E Drop attacks / sudden leg give-way mixed
Key story
Sudden collapse without LOC; rapid recovery; can be vestibular, vertebrobasilar, cataplexy, or mechanical knee/hip.
Approach
Careful neuro + cardiac + vestibular screening; focus on triggers and recurrence pattern.

Step 3: Causes of Falls by Age Group

Older adults Common causes
  • Gait/balance impairment (deconditioning, neuropathy, Parkinsonism)
  • Medications (benzos, opioids, antihypertensives, anticholinergics)
  • Orthostatic hypotension (volume depletion, autonomic dysfunction)
  • Vision impairment
  • Cardiac arrhythmias
  • Vestibular disorders
  • Environmental hazards (loose rugs, poor lighting)
Most older falls are multifactorial. You will usually find 2–4 contributors.
Younger adults Common causes
  • Trauma / sports / intoxication
  • Vasovagal syncope (heat, dehydration, pain, needles)
  • Arrhythmia (rare but critical)
  • Seizure (new-onset, substance-related, structural)
  • Hypoglycemia (diabetes meds, poor intake)
  • Psychogenic/functional (diagnosis of exclusion)

Step 4: “Find the Cause” History (Exact Questions)

1 Before the fall (prodrome)
  • Lightheaded? tunnel vision? nausea/sweating? (vasovagal/orthostatic)
  • Palpitations or chest pain? (arrhythmia/ischemia)
  • New focal neuro symptoms? (stroke)
  • Vertigo? diplopia? ataxia? (posterior circulation/vestibular)
  • Medication/alcohol use? new dose? missed meals? (toxic/hypoglycemia)
2 During the fall (mechanism)
  • Trip/slip? missed step? turning quickly? rising from bed?
  • Head turn triggers? (carotid sinus sensitivity in some older adults)
  • Was there true loss of consciousness?
  • Witness report: stiffening, rhythmic jerking, eye deviation, cyanosis?
3 After the fall (recovery)
  • Immediate recovery (seconds) favors syncope/orthostasis.
  • Prolonged confusion (minutes–hours) favors seizure, head injury, metabolic.
  • Any focal deficit after? (stroke, subdural)
  • Injury pattern: face/head trauma can suggest LOC.
4 Baseline risk & context
  • Prior falls? how many in last 6–12 months?
  • Assistive devices? baseline gait? neuropathy? Parkinsonism?
  • Vision/hearing issues?
  • Home hazards: rugs, stairs, poor lighting
  • Med list focus: sedatives, antihypertensives, diuretics, hypoglycemics

Focused Exam (High Yield)

Vitals Orthostatics + cardiac
  • Orthostatic BP/HR (if safe)
  • Heart exam, volume status
  • ECG for conduction disease, QT, ischemia
Neuro “30-second localization screen”
  • Pupils, extraocular movements, facial symmetry
  • Pronator drift, symmetric strength
  • Finger-to-nose, heel-to-shin, gait if safe
  • Sensation and proprioception (neuropathy clues)
Gait Observe
  • Wide-based? shuffling? freezing? steppage (foot drop)?
  • Timed Up and Go if stable (functional fall risk)

What to Order (Practical)

  • Glucose, CBC, BMP/CMP if unclear cause or recurrent falls
  • ECG (almost always)
  • CT head if head trauma, anticoagulation, new neuro deficits, or persistent AMS
  • Telemetry if syncope suspected or high-risk features
The test list should follow the story. The story is the diagnostic tool.

When to Call Neurology

  • Fall with focal deficits or suspected posterior circulation stroke
  • Suspected seizure or prolonged post-event confusion
  • Recurrent unexplained falls with abnormal neuro exam
  • Concern for Parkinsonism, neuropathy, cerebellar syndrome
