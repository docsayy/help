Intracranial Hemorrhage (ICH)

Immediate stabilization, imaging, reversal, BP control, and when to call neurosurgery

Home Back to Neurology

Core Goal

In suspected intracranial hemorrhage, your job is to: (1) stabilize, (2) confirm with CT, and (3) prevent expansion/secondary brain injury while activating stroke/neurosurgery pathways early.

High-yield rule: CT head first. Then: BP control + anticoag reversal + ICP precautions.

Step 1: Recognize High-Risk Presentations

Red flags Think hemorrhage until proven otherwise
  • Sudden severe headache (“worst of life”) ± meningismus
  • Decreased consciousness, vomiting, or rapidly worsening neuro deficits
  • New focal deficit with very high BP
  • Seizure at onset (can occur in ICH/SAH)
  • Head trauma (especially anticoagulated, elderly, alcohol use)
Do not delay CT for labs unless unstable airway/breathing/circulation requires immediate action.

Step 2: Immediate Actions (First Minutes)

0 Stabilize
  • ABCs, continuous monitoring, large-bore IVs
  • Check glucose (hypoglycemia mimics neuro catastrophe)
  • NPO, aspiration precautions, elevate head of bed ~30°
  • Stop antithrombotics/anticoagulants until clarified
  • STAT non-contrast CT head
Labs “First batch”
  • CBC, CMP, PT/INR, aPTT
  • Type & screen
  • Consider troponin/EKG (stress cardiomyopathy/ischemia can coexist)
  • If on DOAC: document last dose time; specific drug matters
Consult Who to call early
  • Neurology / stroke team
  • Neurosurgery if ICH with mass effect, IVH/hydrocephalus, cerebellar bleed, subdural/epidural, or clinical deterioration
  • ICU for close neuro monitoring

Types of Intracranial Hemorrhage (What CT Usually Shows)

Type Typical clue Classic setting
Intraparenchymal ICH Focal deficit ± headache, vomiting, ↓LOC Hypertension, cerebral amyloid angiopathy, anticoagulation, AVM/tumor
Subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH) Thunderclap headache ± meningismus Ruptured aneurysm (or trauma)
Subdural hematoma (SDH) Fluctuating AMS, headache, focal deficit Bridging vein tear; elderly/anticoag/alcohol; may be delayed
Epidural hematoma (EDH) Trauma, potential “lucid interval” Middle meningeal artery injury; skull fracture
Intraventricular hemorrhage (IVH) Rapid decline, hydrocephalus signs Extension of ICH or primary IVH; needs neurosurg attention
Posterior fossa (cerebellar/brainstem) bleeds can deteriorate fast → early neurosurgery and airway vigilance.

Step 3: Blood Pressure (Prevent Expansion, Preserve Perfusion)

BP Practical approach
  • Use IV titratable agents (common: nicardipine, clevidipine, labetalol) per local protocol.
  • Avoid big BP swings; smooth control is the goal.
  • Think about cerebral perfusion: very low BP can worsen ischemia around the hematoma.
Start BP control early while CT is being arranged—don’t wait for the radiology read if suspicion is high.
Targets What to aim for (general)
General ICH
Many protocols target SBP around 140–160 if presenting SBP is elevated, avoiding hypotension. Follow your institution’s stroke/ICH pathway.
SAH
Avoid severe hypertension (rebleed risk) but maintain perfusion; targets vary—follow protocol.
Traumatic SDH/EDH
Avoid hypotension; neurosurgery often prioritizes perfusion + rapid operative evaluation.
Use local protocol for exact cutoffs—targets can differ by bleed type and neurosurgical plan.

Step 4: Reverse Anticoagulation / Antithrombotics

Reversal is time-sensitive. Match the reversal to the drug.

Warfarin (elevated INR)
  • Give 4-factor PCC + IV vitamin K per protocol.
  • Recheck INR after PCC (timing per institution).
DOACs (apixaban/rivaroxaban/dabigatran)
  • Document last dose time, renal function, and the specific DOAC.
  • Use institution protocol for reversal:
    • Factor Xa inhibitors: andexanet alfa or PCC-based strategies (protocol-dependent)
    • Dabigatran: idarucizumab (when available) ± dialysis in selected cases
If you don’t know the DOAC and time is critical, call pharmacy early—this is a “don’t freestyle” moment.
Heparin
  • Stop infusion; reverse with protamine per protocol if indicated.
Antiplatelets (aspirin/clopidogrel)
  • Stop antiplatelets.
  • Platelet transfusion is not routine for spontaneous ICH (varies by context); may be considered for emergent neurosurgical procedure—follow neurosurgery/stroke protocol.
  • Consider desmopressin (DDAVP) in certain settings per protocol.

Step 5: ICP & Herniation Prevention

Basics “Brain protection bundle”
  • Head of bed 30°, neck midline, avoid tight collars
  • Avoid hypoxia and hypotension; treat fever
  • Maintain normal sodium and glucose; avoid over-sedation masking neuro exam
  • Consider seizure management if suspected/confirmed
Herniation signs Act immediately
  • Declining mental status, unequal pupils, abnormal posturing
  • Cushing response (HTN + bradycardia + irregular respirations)
If herniation is suspected: call ICU/neurosurgery STAT, consider hyperosmolar therapy per protocol, and secure airway if mental status is failing.
Hydrocephalus / IVH
  • Look for ventricular enlargement on CT and rapid decline.
  • Neurosurgery may place an external ventricular drain (EVD) when indicated.

Step 6: When to Get CTA / Look for a Cause

CTA/MRA When to suspect secondary ICH
  • Young patient or no hypertension history
  • Lobar hemorrhage (especially without clear hypertensive pattern)
  • Unusual location, intraventricular prominence, or recurrent hemorrhage
  • Concern for aneurysm/AVM, vasculitis, tumor bleed
Hypertensive ICH often involves deep structures (basal ganglia, thalamus, pons, cerebellum). Lobar bleeds raise amyloid/vascular malformation/tumor questions.

Disposition & Monitoring

  • Frequent neuro checks (timing per ICU/stroke protocol)
  • Repeat imaging if clinical change or per protocol for expansion monitoring
  • DVT prophylaxis timing is individualized; follow neurosurgery/stroke protocol
  • Plan for swallow evaluation and rehab early once stable

Common Pitfalls

  • Labeling as “ischemic stroke” and giving thrombolytics before CT
  • Missing SDH in an elderly anticoagulated patient with fluctuating confusion
  • Over-sedating and losing the ability to trend neuro exam
  • Delaying anticoag reversal while “waiting for labs”
  • Assuming a normal early CT rules out SAH in thunderclap headache (protocol-driven next steps may apply)
When in doubt: CT first, reverse what you can, and escalate early. Hemorrhage doesn’t negotiate.
