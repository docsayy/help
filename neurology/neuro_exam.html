How to Use This Guide

This is a top-to-bottom neurologic exam designed for real bedside use. Each step includes: how to do it, what it tests, and how abnormal findings help localize the lesion.

Localization rule of thumb: UMN signs (hyperreflexia, spasticity, Babinski) suggest brain/spinal cord; LMN signs (atrophy, fasciculations, hyporeflexia) suggest root/nerve.

Stepwise Exam

Step 0 Safety + One-Minute Triage
Do this
  • Check airway/respiratory effort if bulbar symptoms, myasthenia, GBS.
  • Glucose immediately for any focal deficit or AMS.
  • Ask: Last known well and time course (sudden vs progressive).
What it tests
Identifies reversible mimics and high-risk neuromuscular weakness.
Localization clue
Sudden onset suggests vascular; progressive suggests infectious/inflammatory/toxic/metabolic or compressive.
If the story is sudden and focal, treat it like stroke until proven otherwise.
Step 1 Mental Status (Attention, Orientation, Memory)
Do this
  • Level of arousal: alert, drowsy, stuporous.
  • Attention: spell WORLD backward or months backward.
  • Orientation: person/place/time/situation.
  • Immediate recall (3 words), delayed recall (after 3–5 min).
What it tests
Diffuse cortical function and reticular activating system (brainstem arousal network).
Localization clue
Primary attention impairment = delirium/diffuse encephalopathy. Profound arousal impairment suggests brainstem/thalamic involvement or severe systemic illness.
Step 2 Speech & Language (Aphasia vs Dysarthria)
Do this
  • Fluency: conversational speech.
  • Comprehension: follow a 2-step command.
  • Repetition: “No ifs, ands, or buts.”
  • Naming: pen, watch, badge.
  • Articulation: listen for slurring (dysarthria).
What it tests
Dominant hemisphere language network; motor speech pathways for dysarthria.
Localization clue
Aphasia localizes to dominant hemisphere cortex (often MCA). Dysarthria suggests motor speech pathway, brainstem, cerebellum, or peripheral/bulbar weakness.
Aphasia can be severely disabling even with low NIHSS.
Step 3 Cranial Nerves (CN II–XII)
Do this
  • CN II: pupils, visual fields by confrontation, fundus if possible.
  • CN III/IV/VI: EOMs, diplopia, ptosis; check for nystagmus.
  • CN V/VII: facial sensation and facial movements (raise eyebrows, smile).
  • CN VIII: hearing; dizziness context (see HINTS section below).
  • CN IX/X: palate elevation, voice quality, swallow safety.
  • CN XI: shoulder shrug.
  • CN XII: tongue midline, fasciculations.
What it tests
Brainstem nuclei/tracts and peripheral cranial nerves.
Localization clue
“Crossed findings” (ipsilateral cranial nerve deficit with contralateral body weakness/sensory loss) strongly suggests brainstem stroke.
Diplopia + dysarthria + dysphagia + ataxia = posterior circulation until proven otherwise.
Step 4 Motor Exam (Strength, Tone, Drift)
Do this
  • Observe bulk: atrophy? fasciculations?
  • Tone: spastic vs flaccid.
  • Pronator drift: arms out, palms up, eyes closed.
  • Strength: proximal and distal in all limbs (grade if needed).
What it tests
Corticospinal tract, LMN units, and neuromuscular junction/muscle patterns.
Localization clue
UMN pattern: spasticity + hyperreflexia. LMN pattern: atrophy/fasciculations + hyporeflexia. Proximal weakness suggests myopathy; fatigable weakness suggests NMJ.
Pronator drift is one of the fastest sensitive screens for subtle UMN weakness.
Step 5 Reflexes (and Babinski)
Do this
  • Biceps, triceps, brachioradialis, patellar, Achilles.
  • Compare side-to-side.
  • Plantar response (Babinski).
What it tests
Integrity of reflex arcs (LMN) and descending inhibition (UMN).
Localization clue
Hyperreflexia/Babinski = UMN (brain/spinal cord). Hyporeflexia = peripheral nerve/root, early spinal shock, or neuropathy.
Step 6 Sensory Exam (Pain, Vibration, Proprioception)
Do this
  • Light touch + pinprick in limbs.
  • Vibration (tuning fork) at toes/ankles if neuropathy suspected.
  • Proprioception at great toe.
  • Look for a sensory level on trunk if myelopathy suspected.
What it tests
Spinothalamic (pain/temp) and dorsal columns (vibration/proprioception).
Localization clue
Dermatomal pattern suggests root. Stocking-glove suggests peripheral neuropathy. Sensory level suggests spinal cord lesion.
A clear sensory level is spinal cord until proven otherwise—image the spine urgently if new.
Step 7 Coordination (Cerebellar)
Do this
  • Finger–nose–finger and heel–shin.
  • Rapid alternating movements.
  • Check for truncal ataxia (sitting balance).
What it tests
Cerebellum and its connections (often posterior circulation).
Localization clue
Ipsilateral limb ataxia suggests cerebellar hemisphere. Severe gait/truncal ataxia suggests midline cerebellum or vestibular pathways.
Step 8 Gait (and Romberg)
Do this
  • Normal gait, tandem gait (if safe).
  • Heel walk and toe walk (if safe).
  • Romberg: feet together, eyes closed.
What it tests
Integrates strength, sensation, cerebellum, vestibular function.
Localization clue
Romberg positive (falls with eyes closed) suggests proprioceptive loss. Wide-based gait suggests cerebellar. Shuffling suggests parkinsonism.
In “dizzy” patients, inability to walk normally is a major central red flag.

HINTS Exam Only for Acute Vestibular Syndrome

The HINTS exam is valid only in acute vestibular syndrome (continuous vertigo hours–days + spontaneous nystagmus + gait instability). It is not designed for episodic or positional dizziness.

H Head Impulse
How
Patient fixates on your nose; quickly rotate head ~15–20° to each side.
Interpret
Abnormal (corrective saccade) → peripheral. Normal head impulse in AVS → concerning for central stroke.
N Nystagmus
How
Observe primary gaze, then left/right gaze.
Interpret
Unidirectional horizontal → peripheral. Direction-changing, vertical, or pure torsional → central.
T Test of Skew
How
Alternate cover/uncover each eye; look for vertical re-fixation.
Interpret
Vertical corrective movement → central (brainstem) sign.
Any one central sign (normal HIT, direction-changing/vertical nystagmus, or skew) → treat as posterior circulation stroke until proven otherwise.

Ultra-Fast Localization Cheatsheet

  • Cortex: aphasia, neglect, visual field cut, seizures
  • Internal capsule: pure motor stroke (face/arm/leg) with minimal cortical signs
  • Brainstem: crossed findings (CN deficit + contralateral weakness/sensory loss)
  • Spinal cord: sensory level, bilateral weakness, bowel/bladder changes
  • Peripheral nerve: distal weakness + sensory loss in a nerve distribution
  • NMJ: fatigable weakness, ptosis/diplopia, normal sensation
  • Myopathy: proximal weakness, normal sensation, reflexes often preserved until late
