Altered Mental Status (AMS)

Stepwise bedside approach, rapid workup, and “when to call Neuro” logic

Home Back to Neurology

Core Idea

AMS is not one diagnosis. Your job is to quickly decide whether this is: (1) diffuse brain dysfunction (delirium/encephalopathy), (2) focal structural disease (stroke/bleed/mass), or (3) seizures/nonconvulsive status.

Two high-yield questions: Is it focal? Is it abrupt?

Stepwise Algorithm

Step 0 Stabilize + Reversible Mimics (60 seconds)
Do this
  • Airway/breathing/circulation; check O2, vitals, temp.
  • Fingerstick glucose immediately.
  • Consider naloxone if opioid toxicity possible (based on exam/respirations).
  • Look for hypoxia, hypercapnia, shock.
What it’s testing
Life threats + the fastest fixable causes.
Localization clue
If oxygenation/ventilation or perfusion is failing, AMS is often global encephalopathy (until proven otherwise).
If glucose is low, everything else is trivia until corrected.
Step 1 Define the Phenotype: Delirium vs Aphasia vs Coma
Do this
  • Level of arousal: alert → drowsy → stupor → coma.
  • Attention: months backward / WORLD backward.
  • Language screen: naming + simple commands + repetition.
  • Behavior: agitation, hallucinations, disinhibition.
What it’s testing
Diffuse attention network vs focal language cortex vs arousal pathways.
Localization clue
  • Primary attention deficit → delirium/encephalopathy (diffuse).
  • True aphasia → dominant hemisphere cortex (often MCA territory).
  • Coma → brainstem/thalamus or severe systemic/toxic/metabolic.
Step 2 Decide: Structural vs Metabolic/Toxic vs Seizure
Structural clues
  • New focal deficit (face droop, drift, gaze deviation, field cut).
  • Sudden onset or “worst headache.”
  • New asymmetric pupils or abnormal brainstem reflexes.
Metabolic/toxic clues
  • Fluctuating course; inattention; diffuse myoclonus/asterixis.
  • Medication changes, intoxication, withdrawal.
  • Systemic illness (infection, renal/hepatic failure).
Seizure clues
  • Witnessed convulsion, tongue bite, urinary incontinence.
  • Unexplained prolonged post-ictal state.
  • Subtle rhythmic twitching, gaze deviation, unexplained agitation.
If focal + abrupt → assume structural until imaging proves otherwise.
Step 3 The “Must-Not-Miss” Orders Set
Always consider
  • Glucose (already), CBC, CMP, Mg/Phos.
  • VBG/ABG if CO2 retention, severe illness, or unexplained somnolence.
  • UA/urine culture if indicated; tox screen if changes management.
  • EKG; troponin if concern for ischemia/arrhythmia/hypoperfusion.
Imaging
  • CT head if new focal findings, trauma, anticoagulation, severe headache, or unexplained coma.
  • CTA head/neck if stroke suspected or focal neuro signs.
Infectious
  • Blood cultures + lactate if sepsis.
  • If meningitis/encephalitis suspected: treat early; consider LP (after CT if indicated).
Step 4 When to Get EEG Don’t miss NCSE
Get EEG if
  • Unexplained AMS after initial stabilization + basic labs.
  • Prolonged post-ictal state or recurrent unexplained confusion.
  • Unexplained coma, especially in ICU.
  • Subtle motor findings (twitching, eye deviation) or fluctuating mentation.
What EEG answers
Seizure activity (including nonconvulsive status), and degree of encephalopathy.
Localization clue
Focal epileptiform activity may point toward focal structural lesions.
Nonconvulsive status can look like “just delirium” — EEG is how you prove it.
Step 5 When to Do Lumbar Puncture (LP)
LP helps when
  • Fever + AMS (especially with headache, neck stiffness, photophobia).
  • Encephalitis signs: new seizures, focal findings, personality change.
  • Concern for SAH with negative CT but high suspicion (case-dependent).
CT before LP if
  • Focal neuro deficit, papilledema, new seizure, immunocompromised, or significantly altered level of consciousness.
What it answers
Infection/inflammation, hemorrhage patterns, and opening pressure clues.
If encephalitis is suspected, start empiric treatment early—don’t wait for LP perfection.
Step 6 When to Get MRI Brain
MRI helps when
  • CT is unrevealing but focal findings persist.
  • Suspected posterior circulation stroke with normal CT.
  • Concern for encephalitis, demyelination, vasculitis, or occult infarcts.
  • Persistent unexplained AMS after initial workup.
What MRI answers
Small infarcts, brainstem lesions, inflammatory patterns, and diffusion restriction.

Ultra-Fast Localization Cheatsheet

  • Diffuse encephalopathy: primary inattention, fluctuating course, symmetric exam
  • Dominant cortex: aphasia (naming/commands/repetition)
  • Non-dominant cortex: neglect, anosognosia
  • Brainstem: cranial nerves, crossed findings, abnormal pupils/ocular movements
  • Seizure/NCSE: fluctuating confusion, subtle motor signs, prolonged “post-ictal” state

When to Call Neurology Practical triggers

  • Any new focal deficit or suspected stroke/TIA
  • Concern for nonconvulsive status epilepticus (need EEG guidance)
  • Concern for encephalitis, unusual MRI findings, demyelination, vasculitis
  • Unexplained coma or rapidly worsening mental status
