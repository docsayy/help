Altered Mental Status (AMS)

Stepwise bedside approach: stabilize → identify reversible causes → don’t miss stroke/NCSE

What AMS Means (Clinically)

Definition
Change in arousal, attention, cognition, or behavior from baseline.
First rule
AMS is a symptom — your job is to rapidly separate reversible medical/toxic from structural brain.
Reality check: You can’t diagnose “dementia” or “psychiatric” until delirium/medical causes are excluded.

1-Minute Safety Sweep Do first

Step 0 Stabilize + Immediate Reversibles
ABCs
Airway protection, oxygen, suction, monitor, IV access.
Fingerstick
Glucose now. Treat hypoglycemia immediately.
Vitals
Temp, BP/MAP, HR, RR, SpO₂.
Quick neuro
Pupils, gaze, face symmetry, limb drift, speech, neglect.
Never skip: glucose + oxygenation + vitals. They’re the fastest lifesavers.

Common Causes of AMS — and How to Rule Out Each

Use this as your “night-float checklist.” Start at A, move down. Don’t shotgun randomly.

A Immediately Life-Threatening / Rapidly Reversible
Hypoglycemia
Rule out with bedside glucose. Treat first; confirm later.
Hypoxia
SpO₂, ABG/VBG if uncertain. Consider PE, pneumonia, edema, COPD, OSA/OHS.
Hypercapnia
VBG/ABG, clinical CO₂ narcosis (somnolence, headache). Consider COPD/OHS, sedatives.
Shock / Hypoperfusion
Vitals/MAP, lactate, bedside ultrasound if available, evaluate bleeding/sepsis/cardiogenic causes.
Opioid toxicity
Pinpoint pupils, hypoventilation. Rule out with history/meds; respond to naloxone if indicated.
If the patient is sleepy + breathing poorly, think CO₂ and meds before blaming “confusion.”
B Metabolic / Toxic Most common on floors
Electrolytes
BMP/CMP (Na, Ca), add Mg/Phos. Hyponatremia can cause seizures/AMS; hyperNa causes lethargy.
Renal failure / Uremia
BUN/Cr, asterixis, pericarditis/nausea history. Consider dialysis indications if severe.
Hepatic encephalopathy
Clinical diagnosis + LFTs, ammonia supportive (not definitive). Look for triggers: GI bleed, infection, constipation, sedatives.
Medication effect
Full med rec: opioids, benzos, anticholinergics, gabapentinoids, polypharmacy, new meds/dose changes.
Withdrawal
Alcohol/benzo withdrawal: tremor, agitation, autonomic hyperactivity, hallucinosis; confirm with history and vitals.
Tox/ingestion
Consider when story is unclear: tox screen if it changes management; check salicylates/acetaminophen when appropriate.
In older patients, “new AMS” is meds until proven otherwise — especially after pain meds or sleep meds.
C Infection / Inflammation
Sepsis
Vitals, lactate, CBC, cultures, source search. Elderly can be afebrile.
Pneumonia
CXR, oxygenation, exam; consider aspiration.
UTI
UA/UCx when symptoms or systemic signs. Asymptomatic bacteriuria ≠ cause by itself.
Meningitis/Encephalitis
Red flags: fever + neck stiffness + AMS, new seizures, focal deficits. CT head first if indicated → LP. Start empiric therapy if high suspicion.
Do not delay treatment: if meningitis/encephalitis is truly suspected, start empiric antibiotics/antivirals while arranging LP/imaging.
D Structural / Neurologic Don’t miss
ICH / Large stroke
Non-contrast CT head. Think when focal deficit, severe headache, vomiting, anticoagulation, depressed consciousness.
Subdural hematoma
CT head. Elderly, alcohol use, anticoagulated, remote fall; can present subacute with fluctuating AMS.
Seizure / Post-ictal
Witnessed event? Tongue bite/incontinence? Consider if unexplained AMS after shaking or staring spells.
NCSE
EEG is the test. Suspect with persistent unexplained AMS, subtle twitching/eye deviation, prolonged post-ictal, ICU coma.
Increased ICP
CT/MRI; clues: headache, vomiting, papilledema, Cushing physiology.
If AMS is unexplained after glucose/oxygen/labs/CT → think EEG. NCSE hides in plain sight.
E Psychiatric / Functional Diagnosis of exclusion
Primary psychosis / catatonia
Consider only after medical causes are addressed. Catatonia clues: waxy flexibility, mutism, rigidity, negativism.
Dissociative / functional
Normal vitals/labs/imaging, inconsistent exam; still requires careful exclusion of medical illness.
New AMS is medical until proven otherwise. “Psych” is not a first-pass label.

What to Order Early Practical baseline

Labs Common “first batch”
  • Glucose (POC), CBC, CMP/BMP
  • Mg, Phos
  • VBG/ABG if respiratory concern
  • UA ± culture, CXR if infection concern
  • EKG (silent arrhythmia/ischemia can present as “confusion”)
Imaging When to get CT head
  • Focal deficits, new seizure, anticoagulation, head trauma/fall, severe headache, markedly decreased consciousness
  • Concern for hemorrhage, mass, hydrocephalus
Normal CT does not rule out early ischemic posterior stroke — trust the exam.
LP When to consider lumbar puncture
  • Suspected meningitis/encephalitis (fever + AMS, seizures, meningismus)
  • Subarachnoid hemorrhage when CT is negative but suspicion remains (institution dependent)
EEG When to order EEG
  • Persistent unexplained AMS after initial workup
  • Prolonged post-ictal state, subtle motor phenomena, ICU coma
  • Concern for nonconvulsive status epilepticus

Common Pitfalls

  • Stopping at “UTI” based only on bacteriuria without symptoms/systemic signs
  • Ignoring medication changes (opioids/benzos/anticholinergics)
  • Assuming “normal CT” excludes posterior stroke
  • Missing NCSE because there’s no obvious shaking
  • Calling it “dementia” during an acute illness

When to Call Neurology

  • Any focal neuro deficits or concern for stroke/ICH
  • New seizure, recurrent seizures, or suspected NCSE (EEG guidance)
  • Unexplained persistent AMS after basic stabilization + initial workup
  • Concern for encephalitis, unusual presentations, or rapidly progressive decline
