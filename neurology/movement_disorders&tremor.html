How to Think About Movement Problems

Two big buckets: Hypokinetic (too little movement: Parkinsonism) vs Hyperkinetic (too much: tremor/chorea/dystonia/myoclonus).
Start with: When does it happen? (rest, posture, action) + what speed? (rhythmic tremor vs jerky myoclonus vs flowing chorea).

Step 1: Tremor Classification (Highest Yield)

Rest tremor (appears at rest, improves with action)
Think
Parkinson disease (classic “pill-rolling”).
Clues
Unilateral onset, bradykinesia, rigidity, shuffling gait.
Postural / Action tremor (worse holding position or moving)
Most common
Essential tremor (ET) – hands/arms, often bilateral.
Clues
Worse with action, better at rest; family history; improves with alcohol in some.
Red flags
New tremor + meds/thyroid/toxin/withdrawal.
Intention tremor (worse near target)
Think
Cerebellar lesion (stroke, tumor, MS, toxicity).
Clues
Ataxia, dysmetria, nystagmus, wide-based gait.
New cerebellar signs with headache/vomiting or acute onset → urgent imaging.

Step 2: Parkinsonism (Recognize + Don’t Miss Atypical)

Core features Parkinsonism = bradykinesia + (rest tremor or rigidity)
  • Bradykinesia: slow, small movements; reduced arm swing; micrographia
  • Rigidity: cogwheel or lead-pipe
  • Rest tremor may or may not be present
  • Postural instability usually later in typical Parkinson disease
Atypical red flags (think “not classic PD”)
  • Early falls (first year)
  • Early severe autonomic dysfunction (syncope, urinary retention)
  • Early prominent cognitive/behavior change
  • Poor levodopa response
  • Vertical gaze palsy (PSP clue)
  • Marked asymmetric apraxia/alien limb (CBD clue)
If falls are early and dramatic, classic PD is less likely.
Medication-induced parkinsonism (common inpatient problem)
Culprits
Antipsychotics, metoclopramide, prochlorperazine; some antiemetics.
Clue
Often more symmetric; temporal relationship to med exposure.

Other Hyperkinetic Clues (Quick Recognition)

Movement What it looks like Common causes
Myoclonus Sudden, brief “jerks” Uremia, hypoxia, meds (opioids), post-anoxic, seizures
Chorea Flowing, irregular “dance-like” movements Stroke (subthalamic), hyperglycemia, meds, Huntington (rare)
Dystonia Sustained twisting posture Drug-induced, idiopathic, Wilson (young)
Tics Suppressible, urge-driven Chronic tic disorder, Tourette (often younger)
If it’s rhythmic → tremor. If it’s jerky → myoclonus. If it’s flowing → chorea. If it’s twisting → dystonia.

Workup (Practical)

Always consider Reversible causes
  • Medication review (antipsychotics, antiemetics, lithium, valproate, stimulants)
  • TSH (new tremor), CMP (electrolytes), glucose
  • Consider B12 if neuropathy/gait issues
When to image
  • Acute onset movement disorder
  • Focal neuro deficits
  • New cerebellar signs, severe headache, or rapid progression

When to Call Neurology

  • Acute onset chorea/hemiballismus, new cerebellar syndrome
  • Atypical Parkinsonism red flags
  • Disabling tremor not responding to first-line strategies
  • Concern for seizure-related myoclonus or toxic-metabolic jerks
