Stroke Syndromes & Localization

From bedside exam to brain territory in under 60 seconds

Core Mental Model

  • Cortical strokes → higher functions (language, neglect, vision).
  • Subcortical strokes → “pure” motor or sensory syndromes.
  • Brainstem strokes → cranial nerves + long tracts (“crossed findings”).
If you can localize, imaging becomes confirmation—not guesswork.

Cortical Stroke Syndromes

Dominant Hemisphere (usually Left)
Key deficits
  • Aphasia (naming, repetition, comprehension)
  • Alexia, agraphia
Likely vessels
Left MCA > Left ACA
True aphasia is never “just confusion.” It localizes to cortex.
Non-Dominant Hemisphere (usually Right)
Key deficits
  • Left-sided neglect
  • Anosognosia
  • Constructional apraxia
Likely vessels
Right MCA
Neglect is more localizing than weakness.

Lacunar (Subcortical) Stroke Syndromes

Pure Motor Hemiparesis
Findings
Face, arm, and leg weakness without cortical signs.
Lesion
Posterior limb of internal capsule.
Etiology
Small-vessel disease (HTN, diabetes).
Pure Sensory Stroke
Findings
Isolated hemisensory loss.
Lesion
Thalamus.
Ataxic Hemiparesis
Findings
Ipsilateral weakness + ataxia.
Lesion
Internal capsule, pons.
Dysarthria–Clumsy Hand
Findings
Slurred speech + hand clumsiness.
Lesion
Pons or internal capsule.

Brainstem Stroke Localization

General Rule
  • Cranial nerve deficit on one side
  • Motor/sensory deficit on the opposite side
Crossed findings = brainstem until proven otherwise.
Lateral Medullary (Wallenberg)
  • Dysphagia, hoarseness
  • Vertigo, nystagmus
  • Ipsi face pain/temp + contra body pain/temp
PICA stroke → unsafe swallow.
Basilar Artery Occlusion
  • Coma or fluctuating consciousness
  • Quadriparesis
  • Locked-in syndrome (rare)
CTA immediately—this is a neurologic catastrophe.

Ultra-Fast Localization Table

  • Aphasia → dominant cortex (MCA)
  • Neglect → non-dominant cortex
  • Pure motor/sensory → subcortical (lacunar)
  • Crossed signs → brainstem
  • Vision only → PCA
