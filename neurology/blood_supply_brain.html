Purpose of This Guide

Use this page to connect symptoms → brain territory → likely vessel. The goal is not to memorize everything, but to have a reliable mental map during stroke alerts.

One-line rule: Anterior circulation tends to cause weakness/speech; posterior circulation tends to cause dizziness/vision/coordination.

Overview of Cerebral Arterial Supply

The brain receives blood from two paired arterial systems:

  • Internal carotid arteries (anterior circulation)
  • Vertebral arteries (posterior circulation)

These arteries form an anastomotic network at the base of the brain known as the Circle of Willis, from which branches supply the cerebrum, deep structures, brainstem, and cerebellum.

Anterior Circulation Carotid system

Internal Carotid Artery (ICA)
Supplies
Feeds ACA + MCA territories; deep structures via perforators (lenticulostriate arteries).
Classic deficits
  • Large hemispheric stroke pattern
  • Severe contralateral weakness
  • Aphasia (dominant hemisphere) or neglect (non-dominant hemisphere)
  • May have reduced level of consciousness
Common note
ICA terminus occlusions are common LVOs and often thrombectomy targets.
If the deficit is big and the exam looks like “half the brain is offline,” think ICA/M1.
Anterior Cerebral Artery (ACA)
Supplies
Medial frontal/parietal lobes; leg motor & sensory cortex.
Classic deficits
  • Contralateral leg > arm weakness and sensory loss
  • Behavioral changes / abulia
  • Urinary incontinence (can occur)
Leg-predominant weakness with preserved arm strength is your ACA clue.
Middle Cerebral Artery (MCA) Most common
Supplies
Lateral frontal, parietal, temporal lobes; face/arm motor & sensory cortex; language areas (dominant hemisphere).
Classic deficits
  • Contralateral face/arm > leg weakness and sensory loss
  • Aphasia (left/dominant MCA)
  • Neglect (right/non-dominant MCA)
  • Gaze deviation toward the lesion (common in large strokes)
LVO note
MCA M1 (and proximal M2 in many centers) are common thrombectomy targets.
Low NIHSS can still be disabling in MCA strokes (e.g., aphasia or hemianopsia).

Circle of Willis

The Circle of Willis provides potential collateral flow via the anterior and posterior communicating arteries, though it is often anatomically incomplete.

Vertebral & Basilar Arteries

Vertebral arteries arise from the subclavian arteries, ascend through the transverse foramina, and enter the skull via the foramen magnum. They converge to form the basilar artery.

Posterior Circulation Vertebrobasilar system

Vertebral Arteries
Supplies
Brainstem and cerebellum via multiple branches; contributes to basilar artery formation.
Classic deficits
  • Vertigo/dizziness
  • Ataxia
  • Cranial nerve findings (diplopia, dysarthria, dysphagia)
Posterior circulation strokes often present with “weird” mixed symptoms—trust the exam.
PICA (Posterior Inferior Cerebellar Artery)
Supplies
Lateral medulla and inferior cerebellum.
Classic deficits
  • Dysphagia, hoarseness
  • Vertigo, nystagmus
  • Ipsilateral facial pain/temp loss + contralateral body pain/temp loss
Named syndrome
Wallenberg (lateral medullary) syndrome.
If swallowing is suddenly unsafe with vertigo and ataxia, think lateral medulla/PICA.
Basilar Artery High risk
Supplies
Brainstem (pons), cerebellum, thalami (via branches), posterior circulation hub.
Classic deficits
  • Decreased consciousness/coma
  • Quadriparesis or severe weakness
  • Locked-in syndrome (rare but classic)
  • Respiratory compromise
Clinical note
Basilar occlusion is a neurologic emergency; CTA is critical.
“Dizzy + can’t walk + brainstem signs” → CTA now. Basilar strokes do not wait politely.
PCA (Posterior Cerebral Artery)
Supplies
Occipital lobes (visual cortex) and thalamus.
Classic deficits
  • Contralateral homonymous hemianopia
  • Memory impairment/confusion (can occur)
  • Often minimal weakness
Vision loss can be the main symptom—test visual fields deliberately.

Spinal Cord Blood Supply (Brief)

  • Anterior spinal artery → anterior 2/3 of cord
  • Posterior spinal arteries → posterior columns
  • Artery of Adamkiewicz supplies inferior 2/3 of cord

High-Yield Clinical Summary

  • Anterior circulation → weakness and speech deficits
  • Posterior circulation → dizziness, vision loss, ataxia, cranial nerve findings
  • Posterior strokes may have normal early CT
  • Disability matters more than NIHSS score
