Dizziness & Vertigo

Stepwise approach to differentiate peripheral vs central causes (with HINTS)

Home Back to Neurology

Core Idea

“Dizziness” is a symptom, not a diagnosis. The smartest approach is to classify by timing + triggers, then use a targeted exam to decide if this is central (stroke) or peripheral (inner ear).

High-yield rule: In acute continuous vertigo, the bedside eye exam can outperform early CT for posterior stroke suspicion.

Step 1: Classify the Syndrome

A Acute Vestibular Syndrome (AVS) hours–days, continuous
Looks like
  • Continuous vertigo lasting hours–days
  • Nausea/vomiting
  • Gait instability
  • Spontaneous nystagmus
Main job
Differentiate vestibular neuritis vs posterior circulation stroke.
HINTS is designed for AVS, not for brief episodic dizziness.
B Episodic Triggered Vestibular Syndrome seconds–minutes, positional
Looks like
  • Brief vertigo episodes
  • Triggered by head position changes
  • Between episodes: often near-normal
Classic cause
BPPV (benign paroxysmal positional vertigo)
C Spontaneous Episodic Vestibular Syndrome minutes–hours
Looks like
  • Episodes not clearly positional
  • Minutes–hours
  • May have auditory symptoms or migraine features
Common causes
Vestibular migraine, Ménière disease; consider TIA in vascular risk patients.

Step 2: Central Red Flags (Stroke Until Proven Otherwise)

  • New focal deficits: dysarthria, diplopia, dysphagia, limb weakness/numbness
  • Severe gait inability (cannot stand/walk unassisted)
  • Direction-changing or vertical nystagmus
  • New headache/neck pain (consider dissection)
  • High vascular risk + abrupt onset
Big rule: Inability to walk normally is a major central warning sign.

Step 3: The Eye Exam that Matters (HINTS)

HINTS is validated for Acute Vestibular Syndrome (continuous vertigo + spontaneous nystagmus + gait instability). Do not apply it to episodic or positional symptoms.

H Head Impulse Test
How
Patient stares at your nose. Quickly rotate head ~15–20° to each side.
Peripheral pattern
Abnormal head impulse (corrective saccade) → vestibular neuritis likely.
Central warning
Normal head impulse in true AVS → concerning for stroke.
N Nystagmus Pattern
How
Observe in primary gaze, then left/right gaze.
Peripheral pattern
Unidirectional horizontal nystagmus that follows Alexander’s law.
Central warning
Direction-changing gaze-evoked, vertical, or pure torsional nystagmus.
T Test of Skew
How
Cover/uncover each eye; look for vertical re-fixation.
Central warning
Skew deviation (vertical correction) suggests brainstem involvement.
Any one central sign (normal HIT, direction-changing/vertical nystagmus, or skew) → treat as posterior circulation stroke until proven otherwise.

Step 4: Key Peripheral Diagnoses

BPPV (Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo)
Typical story
Brief vertigo triggered by rolling in bed, looking up, bending down.
Exam
Positional nystagmus provoked on Dix–Hallpike (posterior canal most common).
Treatment
Canalith repositioning maneuvers (Epley).
BPPV is positional, brief, and repeatable with maneuvers.
Neuritis Vestibular Neuritis
Typical story
Acute continuous vertigo, nausea, gait instability; often post-viral.
Exam
Unidirectional horizontal nystagmus + abnormal head impulse; no focal neuro deficits.
Treatment
Supportive + vestibular rehab; short-term antiemetics as needed.
Ménière Disease
Typical story
Episodes of vertigo (minutes–hours) + fluctuating hearing loss + tinnitus + aural fullness.
Clue
Auditory symptoms point to peripheral inner ear pathology.
Migraine Vestibular Migraine
Typical story
Episodic vertigo with migraine history or migraine features (photo/phonophobia, headache, aura).
Clue
Neuro exam often normal between attacks.

Imaging: What to Order (Practical)

CT CT Head
  • Often normal early in posterior circulation ischemia
  • Useful to rule out hemorrhage or mass when presentation is unclear
CTA CTA Head/Neck
  • Use when stroke/dissection is suspected
  • Critical in “brainstem symptoms” or severe gait inability
MRI MRI Brain
  • Best for posterior fossa infarcts when CT is unrevealing
  • Use when symptoms persist or exam suggests central cause
If exam screams central, don’t let a normal CT end the workup.

When to Call Neurology

  • Any central red flags or concerning HINTS features
  • Persistent AVS with unclear cause
  • Suspected posterior circulation stroke or dissection
  • Persistent inability to ambulate safely
