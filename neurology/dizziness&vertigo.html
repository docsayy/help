a. What is “Dizziness”?

Dizziness is a symptom, not a diagnosis. Patients use it to describe abnormal sensations involving balance, spatial perception, cerebral perfusion, or consciousness. The clinician’s job is to translate “dizzy” into a physiologic category so the exam and workup become obvious.

Core rule: Do not chase “dizziness.” Classify it first.

2. What Does the Patient Mean by “Dizzy”?

Vertigo (illusion of movement)
Patient says
  • “The room is spinning”
  • “I feel like I’m moving”
  • “Things are rotating/tilting”
Implies
Vestibular system involvement (peripheral inner ear vs central brainstem/cerebellum).
Presyncope (near fainting)
Patient says
  • “I was going to pass out”
  • “Tunnel vision / blacking out”
  • “Lightheaded when standing”
Implies
Global cerebral hypoperfusion (volume, autonomic, cardiac output).
Syncope (true LOC)
Patient says
  • “I passed out”
  • “I lost consciousness”
  • Witnessed collapse
Implies
Transient loss of consciousness (cardiac until proven otherwise).
Disequilibrium (balance / gait problem)
Patient says
  • “I’m unsteady when walking”
  • “I drift/veer”
  • “I don’t trust my legs”
Implies
Gait/postural control disorder (cerebellum, proprioception, vision, basal ganglia, meds).

3. How to Obtain a High-Yield History

Core Questions (fast + high yield)
  • Onset: sudden vs gradual
  • Timing: continuous vs episodic
  • Duration: seconds vs minutes vs hours vs days
  • Triggers: head movement/position, standing, exertion
  • Associated symptoms: neuro (diplopia, dysarthria, weakness), auditory (hearing loss/tinnitus), cardiac (palpitations/CP)
  • Ability to walk: normal vs needs support vs cannot stand
  • Risk factors/meds: stroke/AF, volume depletion, antihypertensives, sedatives, alcohol
Pattern recognition wins: timing + triggers + gait usually classifies dizziness better than “broad labs.”

1. Vertigo Peripheral vs Central

1a Peripheral Vertigo (inner ear)
Typical
  • Intense spinning + prominent nausea/vomiting
  • Often worse with head movement
  • May have hearing symptoms (depending on diagnosis)
  • Usually able to ambulate (uncomfortably)
Nystagmus
Unidirectional horizontal ± torsional; often suppresses with fixation.
Workup
Clinical diagnosis; image only if central features/atypical course.
Dx BPPV
Story
Brief vertigo (seconds) triggered by rolling in bed, looking up, bending.
Exam
Dix–Hallpike (diagnostic positional test: rapidly move from sitting to head-hanging with head turned; provokes vertigo + characteristic nystagmus if posterior canal BPPV).
Treatment
Epley maneuver (canalith repositioning: sequential head/body positions to move displaced otoliths back into the utricle).
Dx Vestibular Neuritis
Story
Acute continuous vertigo (hours–days), severe nausea; often post-viral; no hearing loss.
Exam
Unidirectional nystagmus + abnormal head impulse; no focal neuro deficits.
Workup
Clinical; consider imaging if any central red flag or atypical features.
Dx Ménière Disease
Story
Episodic vertigo (minutes–hours) + fluctuating hearing loss + tinnitus/aural fullness.
Workup
Audiology/ENT; evaluate hearing pattern and recurrence.
Dx Vestibular Migraine
Story
Episodic vertigo ± headache; photophobia/phonophobia/aura; motion sensitivity.
Workup
Clinical diagnosis; neuro exam usually normal between attacks; exclude stroke/TIA when indicated.
1b Central Vertigo (brainstem/cerebellum)
Why dangerous
Often posterior circulation stroke; may have subtle neurologic signs and severe gait instability.
“Subtle brainstem signs”
(minor focal findings that are easy to miss): diplopia, dysarthria, dysphagia, facial sensory changes, limb ataxia, direction-changing nystagmus.
Nystagmus
Vertical, direction-changing gaze-evoked, or pure torsional; fixation suppression unreliable.
Central red flags: focal neuro deficits, inability to walk, vertical/direction-changing nystagmus, skew deviation, new severe headache/neck pain.
AVS Acute Vestibular Syndrome
Definition
Acute Vestibular Syndrome (acute onset of continuous vertigo lasting hours–days with nausea/vomiting, spontaneous nystagmus, and gait instability).
Main job
Differentiate vestibular neuritis vs posterior circulation stroke.
In true AVS, the bedside eye exam can outperform early CT for posterior stroke suspicion.
HINTS Exam (only in true AVS)
Head impulse
Normal in true AVS → central concern; abnormal (catch-up saccade) → peripheral.
Nystagmus
Direction-changing/vertical → central.
Test of skew
Vertical re-fixation on cover/uncover → central.
Workup (when central suspected)
  • MRI brain (DWI) for posterior fossa infarct
  • CTA head/neck if dissection/vascular lesion suspected
  • CT head may be normal early; do not let it end the workup

2. Presyncope

Definition (what it means physiologically)

Presyncope is the sensation of impending loss of consciousness due to transient global cerebral hypoperfusion.

Causes (and the “why”)
  • Orthostatic hypotension: inadequate BP response to standing
  • Arrhythmia: transient drop in cardiac output
  • Structural heart disease: fixed obstruction (e.g., AS) limiting output
  • Volume depletion: low preload
  • Vasovagal: reflex hypotension/bradycardia
Symptoms / Signs (what to look for)
  • Lightheadedness, tunnel vision, diaphoresis, nausea
  • Often worse standing, improved supine
  • May have palpitations (arrhythmia) or exertional component (structural)
Exam & Workup (rule in / rule out)
Exam
Vitals + orthostatics; cardiac exam; volume status; focused neuro screen.
Workup
ECG (mandatory), CBC/BMP as indicated, telemetry if high risk, echo if structural concern.

3. Syncope

Definition

Syncope is sudden, transient loss of consciousness with loss of postural tone, followed by spontaneous recovery.

Causes (high-risk first)
  • Cardiac (dangerous): arrhythmias, aortic stenosis, HCM
  • Reflex: vasovagal, situational (cough, micturition)
  • Orthostatic: volume depletion, autonomic dysfunction, meds
  • Neurologic (rare): seizure, SAH (not typical “syncope”)
Symptoms / Signs (clues)
  • Exertional or supine syncope → cardiac until proven otherwise
  • Sudden collapse without prodrome → arrhythmia concern
  • Post-event confusion, tongue bite, prolonged postictal state → seizure concern
Exam & Workup (rule in / rule out)
Exam
Vitals/orthostatics; cardiac exam; neuro exam after recovery; check trauma.
Workup
ECG in all; telemetry; echo if indicated; labs targeted; neuro imaging only if focal deficits/trauma/SAH concern.

4. Disequilibrium (Balance / Gait Disorder)

Definition

Disequilibrium is imbalance primarily while walking/standing, without a spinning illusion.

Causes
  • Cerebellar: stroke, degenerative, tumor
  • Sensory: peripheral neuropathy (loss of proprioception), B12 deficiency
  • Basal ganglia: Parkinsonism
  • Vision: impaired visual input
  • NPH: gait + cognitive + urinary symptoms
  • Meds/toxins: sedatives, anticonvulsants, alcohol
Exam (what to do at bedside)
  • Observe gait (wide-based? shuffling? sensory “stomping”?)
  • Romberg (sensory vs cerebellar)
  • Proprioception/vibration
  • Cerebellar coordination (finger-nose, heel-shin)
Workup & Imaging (how modalities help)
  • MRI brain if cerebellar/central concern
  • Labs targeted: B12, TSH, CBC/CMP as indicated
  • PT/OT evaluation for safety and rehab
  • Vision/hearing assessment when sensory integration suspected

Imaging Modalities — How They Help Quick Reference

CT CT Head
  • Good for hemorrhage/mass effect when presentation unclear
  • Often insensitive early for posterior circulation ischemia
MRI MRI Brain (DWI)
  • Best for posterior fossa infarcts when exam suggests central cause
  • Use when AVS + central features or persistent concern
CTA CTA Head/Neck
  • Evaluates dissection and major vascular pathology
  • Helpful when brainstem symptoms, severe gait inability, or neck pain
Audiology Hearing Tests
  • Supports peripheral inner ear diagnoses (e.g., Ménière pattern)
  • Helps separate migraine vs Ménière vs other causes
